Objectives:

1: To understand the concept of fluids and fluid statics.

2: To understand kinematic problems such as finding particle paths and streamlines. 3: To understand and solve the equation of conservation of mass and energy.

4: To analyze and solve problems related to laminar and turbulent flows

5: To understand the concept of pipe and channel flows and its applications 6: To understand flow problems related to compressible flows

Course Outcomes (CO):

At the end of the course, the student will be able to:

CO1. Understand the fundamental concepts of viscosity, surface tension, pressure (absolute and gage), flow visualization studies using path-lines, streak-lines, and streamlines.

CO2. Understand the kinematics of fluid particles, including the concepts of local and convective accelerations, stream functions, velocity potentials, vorticity and circulation.

CO3. Apply conservation laws like Bernoulli’s equations, conservation of mass, and momentum to fluid flow problems in engineering applications. Understand the concepts of viscous boundary layers and compute drag and lift coefficients using the theory of boundary layer flows.

CO4. Determine flow rates, pressure changes, minor and major head losses for viscous flows through pipes and power transmission through pipes.

CO5. Analyze and design the most economical section: Rectangular Trapezoidal and Circular sections in channel flow.

Formulate and solve one dimensional compressible fluid flow problems

flows.

problems through manometers.

Objectives:

  1. To develop an understanding of stress and strain analysis in three dimensions.

  2. To study different theories of failure and their applications in design.

  3. To analyze inelastic behavior in bending and torsion.

  4. To understand bending of beams and torsion in non-circular sections.

  5. To introduce students to the basics of the Finite Element Method for solving solid mechanics problems.

Course Outcomes (CO):

At the end of the course, the student will be able to:

CO1. Analyze stress and strain states in three dimensions and apply stress transformation techniques. CO2. Evaluate different theories of failure and determine their application in mechanical design.

CO3. Apply energy methods for analyzing deformations and stresses in structures.

CO4. Understand and solve problems related to the bending of beams and torsion of non-circular cross-sections.

CO5. Gain introductory knowledge of the Finite Element Method and apply it to simple solid mechanics problems.

Unit No.

Topics to be Covered

Learning Outcomes

1.

ANALYSIS OF STRESS AND STRAIN

The 3-dimensional state of stress and strain. Stress tensor. Stress invariants. Principal stresses. Differential equations of equilibrium. Plane stress in cartesian and cylindrical coordinates. Strain tensor. Principal strains. Plane strain state in cartesian and cylindrical coordinates. Generalized hooke's law, application to isotropic materials.


Understanding the three-dimensional state of stress and strain, stress tensors, and equilibrium equations helps in analyzing material behavior under different loading conditions. Learning about principal stresses, strain tensors, and Hooke’s law enhances the ability to evaluate structural stability and material response in isotropic materials.

2.

THEORIES OF FAILURE

Theories of failure (maximum normal stress, maximum normal strain, maximum shear stress, octahedral shear stress, maximum strain energy, maximum distortion energy) and their significance.

AXISYMMETRIC PROBLEMS

Thick-walled cylinders. Compound cylinders. Rotating discs of uniform thickness. Discs of variable thickness. Rotating shafts and cylinders.

Gaining knowledge of various failure theories, including maximum stress, strain, and energy-based criteria, provides the ability to predict structural failure and design safer components. The study of axisymmetric problems such as thick-walled cylinders, rotating discs, and shafts enhances the understanding of stress distribution in

pressure vessels and rotating machinery.

3.

INELASTIC BEHAVIOR IN BENDING AND TORSION

Deformation, stresses and residual stresses in the inelastic range in bending of beams and in torsion of circular bars.

Energy methods.

Strain energy in uniaxial and biaxial loading. Principle of superposition. Maxwell's reciprocal theorem. Castigliano's theorems.

Exploring inelastic deformation, residual stresses in bending and torsion, and energy methods enables deeper insights into material behavior beyond the elastic range. Learning about strain energy principles and theorems like Castigliano’s aids in solving structural problems efficiently and optimizing

designs for strength and durability.

4.

BENDING OF BEAMS

Asymmetrical bending of straight bars. Bending of curved beams. Winkler- bach formula for circumferential stresses.

TORSION

Torsion of bars of non-circular cross-sections. Membrane analogy. Thin rectangular sections. Thin-walled tubes.

Understanding asymmetrical and curved beam bending, along with circumferential stress distribution using Winkler-Bach formulas, helps in designing complex structural elements. The study of torsion in non-circular sections, including membrane analogy and thin-walled tubes, provides a foundation for analyzing torque-carrying members in mechanical and aerospace

applications.

5.

INTRODUCTION TO FINITE ELEMENT METHOD

Direct Stiffness Method with the s1-D elements with examples of Solid Mechanics, Fluid Flow, Heat Transfer, Electrical Networks, and trusses. Introduction to Variational and Weighted Residual Formulations with examples.

Learning about the Direct Stiffness Method and its applications in solid mechanics, fluid flow, and heat transfer enhances computational problem- solving skills. Introduction to variational and weighted residual formulations builds a strong foundation for numerical simulation techniques, enabling the application of FEM in engineering

analysis and design.
